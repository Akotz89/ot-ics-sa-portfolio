4. Objection Handling

"We can't put anything new on the OT network."

The sensors are passive only - SPAN port mirroring. Zero injection, zero active scanning. The sensor receives a copy of traffic; it has no path to send data to your controllers. It's architecturally identical to the SIEM you already have on the IT side.

"Our SIEM already monitors the network."

Your SIEM sees IP addresses and ports. Dragos sees "write multiple registers to PLC address 40001." IT tools can't parse SRTP or EGD - that's not a criticism, it's a design reality. Dragos complements your SIEM; it doesn't replace it.

"How long does the ATO process take?"

Dragos Platform 2.x has a published DISA STIG. That means your ISSO has a documented hardening baseline to review, not a blank spreadsheet. For a PoC, we would confirm whether your process requires an IATT or another test authorization, then provide the STIG checklist, network diagrams, data-flow diagrams, and ports/protocols matrix for customer ISSO review. From my own RMF work: a published STIG can reduce the hardening-evidence burden, but the customer ISSO/AO still owns the authorization decision and schedule.

"Why not Claroty or Nozomi?"

Three differentiators worth knowing:

1. OT threat intelligence is central to the product motion. Dragos publicly tracks named OT threat groups and publishes sector-specific intelligence. In this scenario, that matters because the buyer is asking about nation-state pre-positioning, not only asset inventory.

2. Federal-specific commitment. Dragos launched a dedicated Public Sector subsidiary (Oct 2024), has a published DISA STIG, and publicly describes NSA/CISA participation as trusted advisors in Neighborhood Keeper.

3. Neighborhood Keeper is distinctive in the reviewed public materials. It is an opt-in, anonymized threat telemetry sharing network across Dragos customers. I would verify policy, connectivity, and data-handling fit before positioning it as part of a federal deployment.

"What about supply chain compliance?"

We would validate the final bill of materials through Dragos, Carahsoft, and hardware-vendor supply-chain documentation at quote time. I would identify Section 889, FASCSA, country-of-origin, and provenance evidence needs early so your contracting officer can review the actual sensors, servers, TAPs, and switches before award.

"How does this fit our existing authorization boundary?"

I would recommend discussing a separate authorization boundary and a Moderate-Moderate-High FIPS 199 starting point because availability is the primary mission concern. The passive-only architecture reduces one class of operational risk, but your ISSO/AO decides the final boundary, categorization, and evidence requirements. I'll provide SSP contribution sections and SIA documentation proactively so your ISSO can review early.
