3. OT/ICS Industry & Threat Landscape

Understanding the OT/ICS threat landscape is foundational to the SA role. Federal customers are not buying cybersecurity products — they are defending national critical infrastructure against state-sponsored adversaries who have demonstrated the capability and intent to disrupt physical processes.

The ICS Cyber Kill Chain

Stage 1 IT Network Compromise

Adversary gains initial access through the enterprise network — phishing, supply chain, VPN exploitation, internet-facing assets. Goal: establish foothold and move laterally toward the OT/ICS boundary. This stage mirrors traditional enterprise intrusions.

Stage 2 ICS Attack

Adversary crosses the IT/OT boundary and develops the capability to interact with, disrupt, or destroy the physical process. This requires ICS-specific knowledge: understanding control logic, process dynamics, and safety systems. This is where OT-native detection is essential.

2025-2026 Landscape Trends

Ecosystem-Based Targeting

Adversaries no longer target single facilities. They map entire ecosystems — supply chains, shared protocols, common vendors — to develop scalable attack capabilities that work across sectors.

Edge Device Exploitation

Routers, firewalls, and VPN concentrators at OT network boundaries are increasingly targeted. CISA BOD 26-02 (Feb 2026) mandates federal agencies inventory and replace end-of-support edge devices to mitigate unpatched vulnerabilities.

Ransomware Impact on OT

Dragos tracked 60%+ increase in ransomware incidents impacting industrial organizations in 2024. Even when ransomware doesn't touch OT directly, it forces operational shutdowns.

Federal Regulatory Acceleration

NIST SP 800-82r3, CISA CI Fortify initiative, NSA/CISA OT asset inventory guidance, and new CIRCIA reporting requirements are driving federal OT security investment.

Federal-Specific POC Considerations

ATO / Authorization

Many federal customers require ATO artifacts for any new system on their network. Dragos Platform 2.x has a published DISA STIG, which accelerates the assessment process. I would prepare the ATO artifact package during Phase 2.

Classification Constraints

Classified environments may restrict data export, remote access, and vendor support models. POC design must account for air-gapped operation and on-site-only support. I've operated in these constraints daily.

Passive-Only Requirement

OT environments cannot tolerate active scanning. I would explicitly validate with the customer and their network team that Dragos sensors are passive — SPAN port mirroring only, zero injection. This is non-negotiable.

Multi-Stakeholder Alignment

Federal POCs involve CISO, mission owners, network engineers, ISSOs, contracting officers, and sometimes external assessors. I coordinate across these stakeholders daily in my current role.

Architectural Parallels

Availability-First Operations

My systems support 24/7 national defense operations. Downtime is not an IT inconvenience — it's a mission failure. This is the same mindset as electric grid, water treatment, and manufacturing OT environments.

No-Scan Environments

Active scanning can disrupt my mission systems just as it can crash a PLC. I manage vulnerability assessment through passive observation, careful testing windows, and compensating controls — the exact approach Dragos takes.

Segmented by Design

Four zones, two hard DMZ boundaries, physically isolated circuits. Data flows are controlled, inspected, and logged. This isn't IT "micro-segmentation" — it's real physical and logical isolation with controlled data paths.

Real-Time Sensor Data

I process real-time video, telemetry, and sensor data — low-latency, high-bandwidth, mission-critical. This is the same challenge as SCADA historian data in industrial environments.

"I don't operate PLCs, but I operate a four-zone architecture with two hard firewall boundaries, isolated circuits, protocol translation, and controlled data flows — aircraft control on air-gapped encrypted fiber, mission processing networks behind boundary protection, a collateral operations zone with our SIEM and vulnerability scanning, and another firewall boundary to enterprise. That maps directly to the Purdue Model. The protocols differ, but the architecture, the segmentation philosophy, and the stakes are identical."
